# 202. Nested Transport and Hyperbolic Composition

The double-curl transport law of chapter 7 should not be composed with itself
naively. Applying

$$
\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})
$$

again acts on field structure. It produces a higher spatial operator. It does
not by itself describe one transport riding on another.

The right place to speak of nested transport is not the field operator, but the
transport frame. A simple image is a train and a passenger running inside it.
The train carries one level of organized transport. The passenger adds another
level relative to the moving frame of the train.

Suppose the first transport level advances with speed $v_1$ along some axis,
and the second advances with speed $v_2$ relative to the first. If both levels
are governed by the same invariant transport constant $k$, then the natural
parameter is not speed itself but the rapidity

$$
\eta = \operatorname{artanh}\!\left(\frac{v}{k}\right),
\qquad
v = k\tanh \eta.
$$

Rapidity adds:

$$
\eta_{\mathrm{tot}} = \eta_1 + \eta_2.
$$

Therefore the composed speed is

$$
v_{\mathrm{tot}}
=
k\tanh(\eta_1+\eta_2)
=
\frac{v_1+v_2}{1+v_1v_2/k^2}.
$$

This is the hyperbolic composition law. It is not produced by repeated
application of curl. It appears when one transport process is nested inside
another while the same invariant transport scale $k$ is respected at both
levels.

The conceptual distinction matters:

- double curl organizes source-free transport locally
- repeated double curl changes the structure of the field
- nested transport composes moving frames
- frame composition is hyperbolic when the same invariant transport constant is
  preserved

In this sense, chapter 7 gives the local transport law, while hyperbolic
composition belongs to the kinematics of successive transport levels. The train
and passenger image is therefore useful, but only if the two levels are kept
separate: one is the field law, the other is the composition of moving frames
generated by that law.
