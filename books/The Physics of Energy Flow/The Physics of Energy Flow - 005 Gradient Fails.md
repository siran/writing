# 5. Gradient Fails

**Claim.** The simplest candidate for an evolution law — driving the flux
by a gradient — destroys the divergence-free condition. It is therefore not
a valid description of source-free transport.

Suppose the flux evolved according to

$$
\partial_t \mathbf{F} = \nabla \phi,
$$

for some scalar field $\phi$ constructed from $\mathbf{F}$. Taking the
divergence of both sides:

$$
\partial_t (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}) = \nabla^2 \phi.
$$

The Laplacian $\nabla^2 \phi$ is generically nonzero. This means that even
if $\mathbf{F}$ starts divergence-free, gradient-driven evolution
immediately generates nonzero divergence. Effective sources and sinks appear
dynamically — spontaneously, at every point where $\nabla^2 \phi \neq 0$.

This is not a physical prediction. It is a failure of consistency. Any
evolution law that generates sources in a region declared source-free is
self-contradictory as a description of source-free transport.

Gradient dynamics is therefore ruled out — not by experiment, but by
operator algebra. It cannot do the job.
