# 12c. Extended Galactic Loading and Flat Rotation Curves

Chapter 12 recovered the Newtonian weak-field limit for a compact aggregate
whose far field is well approximated by the scalar monopole of its total
positive energy. Galaxies are not compact spherical aggregates. They are
extended rotating organizations. Their large-scale interaction must therefore
be derived from the full spatial loading profile, not from the point-mass
reduction alone.

This chapter isolates the galactic condition for flat rotation curves and shows
how it arises from extended energy loading. The point is not that a small
$O(1/R^3)$ correction mimics dark matter. That is impossible, because such a
term falls off faster than Newtonian gravity. The point is that an extended
axisymmetric loading can produce a different leading regime altogether.


## 12c.1 Circular motion in the weak scalar field

Write the weak scalar in the galactic midplane as

$$
\eta(R) := \eta(R,z=0),
$$

with cylindrical radius $R$. For a slowly moving circular probe, the radial
acceleration is

$$
a_R(R) = -c^2\,\partial_R\eta(R).
$$

Circular balance requires

$$
\frac{v_c^2(R)}{R}= -c^2\,\partial_R\eta(R),
$$

so

$$
v_c^2(R)= -c^2 R\,\partial_R\eta(R).
$$

For the compact monopole recovered in chapter 12,

$$
\eta(R)=\frac{GM}{Rc^2},
$$

hence

$$
v_c^2(R)=\frac{GM}{R}.
$$

So the compact spherical reduction gives the Newtonian decline. It does not
give a flat galactic curve.


## 12c.2 The exact flat-curve condition

If the rotation curve is flat over some interval,

$$
v_c(R)=v_0,
$$

then the scalar must satisfy

$$
\partial_R\eta(R)= -\frac{v_0^2}{c^2 R}.
$$

Integrating,

$$
\eta(R)=\eta_0-\frac{v_0^2}{c^2}\ln\!\frac{R}{R_0},
$$

for constants $\eta_0$ and $R_0$.

So a flat rotation curve is equivalent to a logarithmic weak scalar over the
relevant galactic range. Equivalently, if one rewrites the same radial field in
Newtonian enclosed-mass form,

$$
\frac{v_c^2(R)}{R}=\frac{GM_{\mathrm{eff}}(R)}{R^2},
$$

then flat rotation means

$$
M_{\mathrm{eff}}(R)=\frac{v_0^2}{G}\,R.
$$

The effective enclosed loading must therefore grow linearly with radius over
the flat region.


## 12c.3 Why the spherical reduction fails for galaxies

The monopole reduction of chapter 12 is valid when the observation distance is
large compared with the full size of the source and the source is effectively
compact. A galactic rotation curve is measured inside the system itself, not in
the asymptotic far field. There the disk geometry still matters.

So there are two distinct regimes:

- compact far field:
  ```math
  \eta(R)\sim \frac{GM}{Rc^2}
  ```
- extended galactic field:
  `\eta(R)` must be computed from the full axisymmetric loading profile

This is exactly where a galactic-scale regime can differ from the compact
Newtonian limit without contradicting it.


## 12c.4 The scale-free disk regime

Let the galaxy carry an effective surface mass-equivalent loading

$$
\Sigma(R)=\frac{\Sigma_0 R_0}{R}
$$

over a substantial radial interval. Here

$$
\Sigma = \frac{\Sigma_E}{c^2}
$$

is the mass-equivalent form of the total organized energy surface density.

For a razor-thin axisymmetric disk, consider the exterior harmonic function

$$
\eta(R,z)
=
\eta_0
-
\frac{v_0^2}{c^2}
\ln\!\left(\frac{\sqrt{R^2+z^2}+|z|}{R_0}\right).
$$

Away from the disk plane this satisfies Laplace's equation. On the plane
$z=0$ it reduces to

$$
\eta(R,0)=\eta_0-\frac{v_0^2}{c^2}\ln\!\frac{R}{R_0},
$$

so

$$
v_c^2(R)= -c^2 R\,\partial_R\eta(R,0)=v_0^2.
$$

Thus the midplane rotation curve is exactly flat.

Now compute the jump across the disk. For $z\to 0^+$,

$$
\partial_z \eta(R,z)\to -\frac{v_0^2}{c^2R},
$$

while for $z\to 0^-$,

$$
\partial_z \eta(R,z)\to +\frac{v_0^2}{c^2R}.
$$

Hence

$$
\bigl[\partial_z\eta\bigr]_{0^-}^{0^+}
=
-\frac{2v_0^2}{c^2R}.
$$

Using the thin-disk jump condition,

$$
\bigl[\partial_z\eta\bigr]_{0^-}^{0^+}
=
-\frac{4\pi G}{c^2}\Sigma(R),
$$

gives

$$
\Sigma(R)=\frac{v_0^2}{2\pi G}\,\frac{1}{R}
=
\frac{\Sigma_0R_0}{R},
$$

with

$$
v_0^2 = 2\pi G \Sigma_0R_0.
$$

So a $1/R$ galactic loading profile gives exactly the logarithmic scalar and
the flat rotation curve.


## 12c.5 What counts as galactic loading

The relevant source is not optical luminosity alone. In this framework the
galactic loading is the total organized electromagnetic energy that contributes
to the large-scale retarded constitutive response.

That can include:

- non-visible radiation
- plasma and magnetic-field energy
- bounded standing closures that are not optically luminous
- a collective refractive medium generated by many closures

So one should consider light we do not see, but not only that. The relevant
object is the full galactic energy-flow organization, not the visible band by
itself.


## 12c.6 Three ways the flat regime can arise

The derivation above says what is needed mathematically: an effective loading
that behaves like

$$
\Sigma(R)\propto \frac{1}{R}
$$

over the galactic range. In this ontology, there are three possible ways that
can happen.

First, the galaxy may itself carry an extended non-pointlike energy profile.
Then the visible and non-visible organized energy already delays the onset of
the compact monopole regime.

Second, many closures may generate a collective refractive medium. In that
case the effective loading relevant for transport is not just the directly
luminous distribution, but a broader medium produced by the same closures in
aggregate.

Third, the simple spherical weak-field reduction can fail across the galactic
disk even when it remains valid asymptotically far outside the galaxy. Then
the flat regime is an interior scale-free disk regime, while the exterior of
the full galaxy can still return to the compact $1/R$ potential.


## 12c.7 Interpretation

The Newtonian limit of chapter 12 is therefore not discarded. It is the compact
far-field limit of the same theory.

Flat galactic rotation curves require a different regime:

- not a faster-decaying correction such as $O(1/R^3)$
- but an extended galactic loading profile
- equivalently, a logarithmic weak scalar over the measured radial range

So the dark-matter question is reformulated here. The issue is not whether an
invisible new substance must be inserted. The issue is whether the total
galactic energy-flow organization, including non-visible radiation and
collective refractive loading, produces the effective $1/R$ surface profile
required by the flat-curve regime.
