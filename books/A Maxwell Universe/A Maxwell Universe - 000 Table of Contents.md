# PART I — FOUNDATIONS OF REALITY

## 1. Darkness : cause–effect as the only primitive

“There is only cause-and-effect relations… these relations define everything
that follows.”

Reality begins in darkness: no space, no time, no geometry, no matter. Only a
single stable Node whose internal structure cannot be fully accessed or
described. Within this Node, subnodes influence one another. A cause produces an
effect. Nothing else is assumed.

From this lone primitive, an ordering appears. Whenever one subnode affects
another, we say it comes “before” in the emergent sense of time. Chains of such
interactions create sequences; closed chains create clocks. Time is not given.
It is inferred from repeated causal loops.

Distance arises in the same way. To move from subnode \(i\) to subnode \(j\) is
to traverse a series of causal steps. Counting these steps yields a notion of
separation, long before any geometry or dimension is imposed. What we later call
space is simply the structure reconstructed from these counts.

This chapter establishes the substrate on which the entire Maxwell Universe is
built. No laws, fields, or dimensions are fundamental. Only the pattern of cause
and effect is. Everything else—time, distance, space, matter—emerges from this
silent ordering hidden within the darkness.

*For a formal development of this framework, see:* [A Cause-Effect Model for Emergent Time and Distance](https://preferredframe.com/prints/doi/10.5281/zenodo.17613402/)


## 2. Emergent Space : distance from causal steps

“Distance is the count of causal steps… space arises from distances.”

If time emerges from the ordering of cause and effect, then space emerges from
the effort required for one subnode to influence another. A causal chain is a
path. Its length—the number of steps—defines a separation. Nothing else is
needed.

In the Node framework, no geometry is assumed. There is only the minimal chain
linking \(n_i\) to \(n_j\). If the shortest sequence of cause–effect relations
has length \(L_{ij}\), we call this the distance between the two subnodes. When
no direct path exists, the distance is undefined or effectively infinite. A
subnode may measure its own separation from others by observing how many causal
steps are required for an influence to return as a closed loop.

This notion of distance does not rely on metrics, coordinates, or dimension. It
is a purely relational quantity. Yet, when many such distances coexist and
interact, observers may approximate them by embedding the causal structure into
a space that best preserves these step counts. Only then does geometry appear.
Only then do we speak of “space.”

Thus spatial structure is not given. It is inferred. What seems like a
continuous manifold is merely the smoothest representation of countless causal
paths. Distance is primitive; geometry is a reconstruction.

## 3. Universal Causal Substrate : any dimension, dense subnodes, all consistent theories encodable

“Given a dense set of subnodes, any causal pattern can be represented… dimension
emerges as a best-fit description, not a primitive one.”

Once time and distance arise from causal steps, the Node may support patterns of
interaction far richer than simple chains. Its subnodes can be densely
interconnected, with many possible routes between them. In such a substrate, no
geometry is fundamental. What looks like a dimension is merely the observer’s
attempt to embed these causal distances into a smooth space with minimal
distortion.

Because the subnodes can carry internal states drawn from any mathematical
structure—real numbers, complex numbers, vectors, or more abstract objects—the
Node can encode the dynamics of any theory that respects causal updating. If a
law of physics can be expressed as “this change produces that change,” it can be
implemented as a rule acting on the subnodes and their causal links. The Node is
universal because cause–effect rules are the only requirement.

Maxwell-like behavior illustrates the point. In experiments, we summarize the
interdependence of electric and magnetic changes through equations such as “∂E
causes B” and “∂B causes E.” In the Node framework, these relations are not
primitive fields living in a background space. They are concise ways of
describing stable causal patterns among subnodes whose interactions happen to
fit a wave-like abstraction.

Thus the substrate can represent any consistent physical theory. Geometry and
field behavior emerge only as compressions of deeper causal activity. Dimension
is chosen by the observer, not imposed by the Node.

## 4. Time Forks : point-mass, field, and spiritual ontologies

“The order in which ontologies arise changes the world that is built from them…
different causal framings generate different realities.”

Once time and distance emerge from causal steps, observers may impose different
interpretations on the same underlying substrate. These interpretations become
ontologies: ways of compressing causal relations into stories about the world.
Nothing guarantees that only one ontology is possible. The causal structure
admits many.

Early human interpretations emphasized non-material influences and intentional
forces. These are spiritual ontologies: causal patterns understood through
meaning, not geometry. Later, with experimental refinement, electromagnetism
became the dominant abstraction. Change in one quantity produces change in
another. Fields replaced intentions, yet both interpret the same underlying
cause–effect substrate.

A similar fork appears in gravity. If mass is taken as fundamental, one arrives
at the Newtonian and relativistic lineage: point-masses, curvature, patches of
quantum rules. If, instead, one starts from electromagnetic configurations and
energy distributions, a different ontology emerges—one in which gravity arises
from wave interactions and refractive geometry rather than from mass or
spacetime curvature.

These ontologies do not contradict each other; they highlight different
regularities in the same causal network. The Node does not choose between them.
Observers do, based on which patterns they compress into laws. Different
starting points lead to different worlds, each consistent in its own framing.

This chapter marks the transition from the point-mass ontology to the Maxwell
ontology explored in Part III.


# PART II — THE POINT-MASS PATH

## 5A. Newtonian Lineage : Newton → Ether → Michelson–Morley → SR/GR → Quantum patches

“The point-mass picture was assumed fundamental… and later required additional
layers—relativity, quantum rules—to patch inconsistencies.”

If one begins with the assumption that matter consists of point-like masses
moving through a pre-existing space, a particular lineage of physics follows.
Newton’s laws define interactions as forces between points. Ether theories were
added to support wave propagation, then discarded when experiments failed to
detect a preferred frame. Relativity replaced the ether with geometry, treating
space and time as a unified background through which point-masses move along
curved paths.

Quantum theory introduced yet another layer: rules to handle discreteness,
probabilities, and measurement, none of which follow naturally from the
point-mass picture. Each new framework patched gaps in the previous one,
preserving the core assumption that matter is made of localized points and that
space is a fixed arena those points inhabit.

This lineage is coherent, but it is not unique. It reflects the commitment to a
point-based ontology rather than a necessity imposed by the causal substrate.


## 5B. Fragmentation : mechanics, relativity, quantum split into branches

“Mechanics, relativity, and quantum theory arise as separate branches because
the underlying ontology was never unified.”

When point-masses are taken as fundamental, each physical domain requires its
own rules. Classical mechanics handles slow, heavy objects. Relativity handles
high-speed motion and gravity. Quantum theory handles microscopic behavior and
probabilities. Nothing ties these together at the root. Each branch explains a
different regime, but none derives the others.

The fragmentation reflects the limitations of the initial assumption. A world
built from points in a background space cannot seamlessly produce wave-like
behavior, curvature, and quantization without introducing new layers of
interpretation. These layers multiply and diverge because the ontology
underneath them is not unified.

In the Node framework, this fragmentation is expected: different ontologies
arise when observers compress different subsets of causal relations into
different forms of law. The point-mass ontology generates mechanics, relativity,
and quantum theory as separate branches. The Maxwell ontology, explored next,
unifies them through energy flow and wave-like causal patterns.

## 8. Photon as Torus : opposing circulations; decomposition

“Opposing circulations in a toroidal EM configuration generate a standing-wave
photon… with internal counter-rotating energy loops.”

In the Maxwell ontology, every stable object is a self-organized pattern of
electromagnetic flow on the causal substrate. The simplest such pattern is a
single toroidal loop. A torus allows circulation around two directions
simultaneously: poloidal and toroidal. When the flows in these directions
balance, a stable standing wave forms. This is the photon in its self-contained
form.

A standing wave is fundamentally two counter-propagating waves. On a torus, this
means two circulations carrying equal energy but opposite orientation. Their
superposition traps the field into a closed, non-dispersive loop. The photon’s
energy, momentum, and polarization arise from the geometry and winding numbers
of this configuration.

This structure also explains how a photon can decompose. If the balance between
the opposing circulations is disturbed, the toroidal pattern can open into
separate counter-propagating components. These components can evolve into an
electron–positron pair: two stable, charged configurations derived from the
unraveling of a single toroidal standing wave.

In this picture, photons are not point particles or excitations of a quantized
field. They are topological wave loops whose stability comes from the closed
geometry of the torus and the symmetry of their internal flows.

## 9. Toroidal Shells : (m,n) flows, 1/r² decay, topological charge

“(m,n) winding flows create layered toroidal shells whose energy density decays

A single toroidal flow defines the simplest stable configuration, but more

Together, (m, n) label the full topological pattern of the electromagnetic

local deformations; they are global properties of the flow.

A configuration with higher (m, n) produces additional layers—toroidal shells—
built from nested loops of field lines. Each shell carries its own energy

1/r². This scaling follows from the geometry of the flow: as the radius grows,
the same circulation spreads over a larger loop, lowering its density while
preserving its topological content.

These toroidal shells behave as structured, quantized energy distributions. The
topological index (m, n) plays the role of a conserved charge. When
configurations interact, their winding numbers combine or redistribute, but
cannot be created or destroyed by smooth evolution. This provides a natural,
purely electromagnetic origin for conserved quantities usually introduced as
independent attributes.

In the Maxwell ontology, matter is not a point with attached quantum numbers. It
is a layered electromagnetic structure whose topological charges arise from the
shape and count of its internal flows. The familiar 1/r² behavior of many fields
is simply the geometric decay of these expanding toroidal shells.

# PART IV — QUANTUM WITHOUT QUANTUM

## 10. No Quantum, No Collapse : PCF deterministic branching

“There is no collapse; the field branches deterministically… all outcomes are
energy partitions of one real field.”

In a Maxwell universe, the world is one continuous electromagnetic field
evolving on the causal substrate. Nothing ever jumps, splits, or collapses.
Instead, the field explores multiple compatible configurations simultaneously,
each carrying a fraction of the total energy. What we call “measurement
outcomes’’ are simply the stable partitions of this energy as it flows through
different causal channels.

The Programmable Collapse Filter (PCF) formalism captures this idea. Instead of
a probabilistic collapse, the field branches deterministically according to the
available modes of interaction. Each branch receives a share of the energy based
on the constraints imposed by the environment. The observer does not choose the
outcome; the environment selects which branches can persist.

In this ontology, probability is not fundamental. It is a bookkeeping device for
tracking how energy distributes across branches. Collapse is not an event. It is
the pruning of unsupported modes as the field interacts with finite resources.
Once a branch stabilizes, it appears as a classical outcome, even though the
underlying process was fully continuous.

This approach removes the conceptual tensions of quantum measurement. There is
only one field, evolving under deterministic rules. Branches form when different
modes propagate through different causal pathways. The apparent indeterminacy is
a reflection of incomplete information about how the environment constrains
these flows.

This perspective unifies classical and quantum behavior. High-frequency

## 12. Energy Fractions : probability as energy distribution

“Probability is the fraction of total field energy in each branch… the Born rule
is energy accounting.”

In a deterministic Maxwell universe, the field never collapses. It divides its
energy among the available causal pathways. Each branch of evolution carries a
portion of the total energy, determined by how the field modes fit the local
constraints. What observers call “probabilities’’ are these fractions.

If a standing mode admits two or more stable continuations, the field
automatically partitions its energy according to how well each continuation
matches the environment. This weighting emerges from the internal structure of
the waves themselves. No randomness is introduced; the distribution follows from
the geometry of the interaction.

The Born rule appears because the energy carried by a mode is proportional to
the square of its amplitude. When the field encounters a branching point,
amplitudes redistribute, and the squared magnitudes of those amplitudes give the
fraction of energy that flows into each branch. Observers interpret these
fractions as probabilities, though nothing probabilistic occurs at the
fundamental level.

Thus probability is not a primitive feature of nature. It arises from energy
partitioning in a continuous electromagnetic field evolving on the causal
substrate. Measurement outcomes are simply those branches that stabilize under
interaction, carrying enough energy to persist while others fade.

## 13. Finite Environments : deviations from Born statistics

“In a finite-mode environment the branch energies fluctuate… producing
measurable deviations from Born statistics.”

The Born rule emerges when a field interacts with an effectively infinite
environment—one capable of absorbing or redistributing energy without
significant back-reaction. But no real environment is infinite. Every measuring
device, every absorber, every coupling region has a finite number of modes. This
finiteness introduces fluctuations in how energy partitions among branches.

When the environment has limited capacity, the field cannot distribute its
energy with perfect smoothness. Small imbalances accumulate. Branches that would
receive an idealized fraction |ψ|² may instead acquire slightly more or less
energy depending on the available mode structure. These deviations are not
violations of quantum mechanics. They are signatures of the underlying
deterministic dynamics responding to finite constraints.

In a finite environment, branch formation is granular. Each mode can only take a
discrete amount of energy before saturating or redirecting flow. As a result,
the squared-amplitude rule becomes an approximation: accurate when many modes
participate, imperfect when the environment is small or structured. Experiments
that isolate systems or restrict coupling pathways should therefore observe
systematic departures from Born statistics.

This perspective reframes quantum probability as a thermodynamic limit. The Born
rule is the smooth, large-environment behavior of a more detailed energy
accounting process. Deviations arise naturally when the environment cannot act
as an infinite reservoir.

# PART V — GRAVITATION & LARGE-SCALE PHYSICS

## 14. Energy–Energy as Gravity : refractive geometry

“Energy modifies the local propagation speed… gradients of energy density act as
gravitational geometry.”

If all matter is electromagnetic structure, then every interaction between
objects is ultimately an interaction between energy distributions. In this
ontology, gravity is not a curvature of spacetime and not a force between
masses. It is the response of wave propagation to variations in field energy.

Electromagnetic waves travel more slowly in regions of high energy density. The
substrate behaves as a refractive medium: energy changes the effective index
along a causal link. When two configurations approach each other, their fields
overlap and modify the local propagation speed. The resulting gradient in
refractive index bends trajectories, concentrating flow toward the region of
higher energy. This bending appears, at large scales, as gravitational
attraction.

The familiar 1/r² behavior arises naturally. As the field of one configuration
extends outward, its energy density decays approximately as 1/r². This creates a
smooth refractive gradient. A passing mode curves toward the denser region with
a strength proportional to that gradient. Newton’s law is the coarse description
of this wave-guided geometry.

In this picture, gravitational lensing, orbital motion, and free fall all follow
from the same principle: waves move toward slower regions. Mass is not the
cause; mass is a derived property, an effective measure of how much a
configuration slows propagation near itself.

At cosmological scales, variations in large-scale energy flow produce apparent
effects attributed to dark matter and dark energy. These arise not from unseen
substances, but from how orthogonal or residual energy modes reshape the
effective refractive geometry of the substrate.

# PART VI — TOPOLOGY, MIND, AND EMERGENCE

## 16. In–Out Meta-Structure : inversion loop and self-reference

The distinction between “in” and “out” is not fundamental. It arises from how a
closed oscillation re-enters itself. In the PNP scalar-field framework, the only
ontic quantity is a real scalar field $U(x,t)$, and orientation appears only as
a projection of its phase gradients. The minimal closed mode, the $(1)$ mode,
shows how inward and outward flow emerge from a single self-referential loop.

A simple standing wave
$$
U(r,t) = A \sin(kr - \omega t), \qquad kR = \pi
$$
cancels at the center and reappears with opposite phase. The field flows inward,
passes through a node where its amplitude vanishes, and returns outward with a
reversed gradient. Defining
$$
\hat{n}(r) = \frac{\nabla U}{|\nabla U|}
$$
one finds a continuous phase evolution but a flipped directional projection:
$$
\lim_{r\to 0^-} \hat{n} = -\lim_{r\to 0^+} \hat{n}.
$$
This is a Möbius-like inversion in direction space, not in the underlying field.
“In’’ and “out’’ are labels assigned to opposite halves of a single continuous
oscillation.

This inversion loop is the minimal self-referential structure supported by the
substrate. It does not presuppose geometry. Spatial orientation is a derived
feature, arising from how phase gradients map onto directional vectors. The
system distinguishes “inside’’ and “outside’’ only because half of the cycle
returns to influence the region that launched it.

The same structure appears at larger scales. Biological breathing cycles follow
an identical pattern: a continuous variable crosses a node where the effective
flow reverses sign. Cognitive systems use the same recursion. A signal departs,
transforms, and returns as input to its origin; part of the loop is interpreted
as self, part as world. Perceived externality is a projection of an internal
oscillation, not a primitive separation.

Across physical, biological, and cognitive levels, the in–out loop is the
simplest closed pattern compatible with stability and recurrence. It links local
and global behavior and provides the structural basis for systems capable of
persistence and self-reference.

## 17. Mode Aggregation : collective (m,n) frequencies and black-body spectrum

A single toroidal mode is a stable electromagnetic pattern labeled by integers
$(m,n)$, counting its independent windings. Real systems, however, rarely exist
as isolated modes. They form aggregates: many modes coexisting, interacting, and
exchanging energy within the same causal environment. When these modes overlap,
their frequencies combine into a dense spectrum of collective oscillations.

Each individual mode has a discrete energy set by its topology. But an ensemble
of modes with different $(m,n)$ produces a broad distribution. As the number of
available modes grows, the aggregated spectrum approaches a continuous curve.
The familiar black-body profile arises when many such modes share energy under
conservation constraints. No quantization rule is required; discreteness comes
from topology, while continuity comes from aggregation.

In a cavity or bounded region, modes fill available causal pathways. Their
interference redistributes energy until a stable ensemble emerges. The resulting
spectrum depends only on the density of states and the capacity of the system to
store energy across different winding configurations. The peak frequency grows
with temperature because higher-energy modes are more easily populated when more
energy is available to the ensemble.

The black-body law therefore appears as a macroscopic limit of electromagnetic
mode aggregation. Each mode is discrete; the ensemble is continuous. The
Planckian curve is the statistical shape formed by many interacting $(m,n)$
structures sharing energy in a stable Node. Classical electrodynamics suffices
when the underlying substrate supports a large number of available modes.

In this view, thermal radiation is not fundamentally quantum. It is the smooth
distribution of energy across a dense hierarchy of topological EM modes.


# PART VII — THERMODYNAMICS & COSMOLOGY

## 18. Thermodynamics and End-State : dipole minimum; Gaussian flux = 0

Thermal behavior in a Maxwell universe arises from how electromagnetic
configurations exchange and redistribute energy across many interacting modes.
As energy flows, higher-order structures tend to decay into simpler, more stable
ones. Among all closed EM configurations, the dipole is the minimal stable
shape. It concentrates energy into two opposed regions with a balanced field
structure and no net Gaussian flux.

A configuration with nonzero net flux must extend outward indefinitely. Such
structures dissipate or reorganize as they interact with their surroundings.
Only those with zero total flux can persist without radiating away energy. The
dipole achieves this: its outward field is exactly balanced by an inward return
structure, yielding a net flux of zero through any closed surface. This makes it
the natural end-state of EM relaxation.

Thermodynamically, energy spreads through mode coupling. Complex topologies
break into simpler ones; shells collapse or merge; standing waves shed energy
