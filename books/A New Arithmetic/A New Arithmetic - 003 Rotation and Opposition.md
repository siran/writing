# Rotation and Opposition

An ultrareal number is a positive square-form:

$$
U = u^2
$$

with $u$ real. But the inner state that exposes it can carry orientation.

To rotate is to turn. To return is to re-turn: the value comes back through a
turn in the inner layer. The standard mathematical language for this is the
complex-analytic toolset built from Euler's notation. Ultrareals extend that
language because the square-form already makes inner orientation operational.

The simplest orientation is sign:

$$
u,\qquad -u
$$

Both expose the same ultrareal:

$$
u^2 = (-u)^2
$$

So sign is not visible as a negative magnitude. It is visible only when inner
values are joined.

## Opposition

If two inner states are opposed, their joined value is:

$$
u^2 + (-v)^2 := (u - v)^2
$$

Expansion gives:

$$
(u - v)^2 = u^2 + v^2 - 2uv
$$

The minus sign appears in the relation term. It does not create a negative
ultrareal.

The special case of perfect opposition is cancellation:

$$
u^2 + (-u)^2 := (u - u)^2 = 0
$$

The result is not less than zero. It is absence after opposition.

## Rotated Minus-Signed Values

A minus-signed value is represented by a rotation out of the ultrareal layer:

$$
-U = (iu)^2
$$

because:

$$
(iu)^2 = i^2u^2 = -u^2
$$

So a minus-signed number is not a negative ultrareal. **There are no negative ultrareals.**
It is a rotated square-value.

The symbol $i$ marks that rotation. It does not mean the positive value has
disappeared. It means the square-value is returning, re-turning, through the
inner layer from another direction:

$$
U = u^2,\qquad -U = (iu)^2.
$$

## Rotated Infinity

The same rule applies at infinity.

Positive infinity is the unbounded limit of positive square-forms:

$$
U = u^2,\qquad
u \to \infty,\qquad
U \to \infty.
$$

Minus-signed infinity is not a different kind of substance. It is the same
unbounded positive square-form seen through the rotated branch:

$$
-U = (iu)^2,\qquad
u \to \infty,\qquad
-U \to -\infty.
$$

So:

$$
-\infty = \text{rotated infinity}
$$

In the ultrareal layer there is only positive unbounded value. The minus sign
belongs to orientation.

## Euler's Rotation

Euler's identity gives the standard notation for this turn:

$$
e^{i\theta} = \cos(\theta) + i\sin(\theta)
$$

This expression represents a point on the unit circle in the complex plane.
Changing $\theta$ rotates the point. Mathematically, notation-wise, and
operationally, this is the calculus of turns used here: complex exponentials
track orientation in the inner layer, and the ultrareal square-form tells how
that orientation returns as visible value.

At a quarter-turn:

$$
\theta = \frac{\pi}{2},\qquad e^{i\pi/2} = i.
$$

So:

$$
i = e^{i\pi/2}
$$

Squaring $i$ doubles the rotation:

$$
i^2 = e^{i\pi} = -1
$$

Therefore:

$$
i = \sqrt{-1}
$$

more precisely:

$$
\sqrt{-1} = \pm i
$$

This is the arithmetic reason minus-signed values can be understood as rotated
positive square-values. The minus sign is a half-turn in value-space,
produced by a quarter-turn in the inner square-root layer.

Opposition is different from a rotated minus-signed value. Opposed values can
both be positive ultrareals; the opposition is their relative turn.

## General Rotation

Let the inner state be rotated:

$$
a = ue^{i\theta}
$$

Then:

$$
a^2 = u^2 e^{i2\theta}
$$

The outer orientation is doubled. A quarter-turn of the inner state becomes a
half-turn of the squared value:

$$
\theta = \frac{\pi}{2},\qquad
(ue^{i\pi/2})^2 = -u^2.
$$

This is why a square-value can carry a minus sign if $u$ is not real. The
minus-signed square is not an ultrareal value; it is the result of rotating the
inner state before squaring.

## Rotation-Aware Joining

If two inner states carry orientations,

$$
a = ue^{i\alpha},\qquad b = ve^{i\beta},
$$

then their positive joined value is:

$$
|a + b|^2
$$

Expanded:

$$
|a + b|^2 = u^2 + v^2 + 2uv\cos(\alpha - \beta)
$$

The relation term depends on relative orientation.

For oriented quantities, the relative orientation can be summarized by one
relation descriptor:

$$
d=\cos\phi,\qquad \phi=\alpha-\beta.
$$

In Euler notation the same turn is carried by $e^{i\phi}$. The symbol $i\phi$
does not introduce a negative ultrareal. It records the operational turn whose
projection gives the real descriptor $d$. This is the vectorial or phase-like
case. Other ultrareal quantities may use hyperbolic, tangential, or otherwise
non-angular descriptors.

Aligned joining:

$$
\alpha - \beta = 0,\qquad |a + b|^2 = (u + v)^2.
$$

Opposed joining:

$$
\alpha - \beta = \pi,\qquad |a + b|^2 = (u - v)^2.
$$

Here both incoming ultrareals are positive. The opposition is the turn between
their inner orientations, not a negative ultrareal.

Orthogonal joining:

$$
\alpha - \beta = \frac{\pi}{2},\qquad |a + b|^2 = u^2 + v^2.
$$

Ordinary addition is therefore the non-interaction case of the operation. It is
the case where the relation term is zero.
