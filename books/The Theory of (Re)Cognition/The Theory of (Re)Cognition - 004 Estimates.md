# Estimates

Finite recognition does not begin with exactness.

It begins with estimates.

This is not a weakness to be repaired later. It is the normal condition of any
loop that must recognize a world it cannot copy whole, cannot view from
nowhere, and cannot exhaust in one encounter.

Once chapter 3 made clear that encoding is not copying, this chapter becomes
necessary. If a loop encodes only partially, across limited exposure and with
its own selective organization, then what it carries forward is never the whole
of what is. It is an estimate of what matters, enough to recognize, enough to
distinguish, enough to steer.


## Estimate Is the Native Form of Finite Knowing

An estimate is not merely a numerical guess.

It is any provisional organization by which a loop treats a pattern as
sufficiently this rather than that.

To estimate is:

- to settle provisionally,
- to preserve enough relation to act,
- to remain open to correction.

So when a loop recognizes something, it usually does not first possess a final
definition of it. It carries a working estimate.

A child has an estimate of danger before a theory of danger. A musician has an
estimate of when a phrase will resolve before any explicit analysis. A driver
has an estimate of distance before measurement. A scientist often has an
estimate of what a signal means before a full theory stabilizes it.

Estimate is therefore not the failure of knowing. It is the first usable form
of knowing available to finite recognizers.


## Recognition Proceeds by Sufficiency, Not Exhaustion

This follows from the earlier chapters.

If recognition is selective resonance, then a loop never takes up everything.
It takes up what matches strongly enough to accumulate and guide later action.

If encoding is induced reorganization, then the loop never carries the whole
encounter forward. It carries a transformed remainder that preserves enough
organization for later use.

So recognition always proceeds by sufficiency, not exhaustion.

The question is not:

> has the loop captured the whole of the pattern?

but:

> has it captured enough of the pattern to recognize, discriminate, and steer?

That "enough" is the domain of estimate.


## Some Estimates Are Rough, Some Are Fine

Not all estimates are equal.

Some are coarse and immediate. Others are refined and patient. Some are bodily,
others linguistic. Some are inherited from culture. Some are forced by
repeated friction with the world.

A rough estimate may still be good enough for survival. A finer estimate may be
required for science, design, law, medicine, or long-term coordination.

So the relevant distinction is not:

- estimate versus truth

but:

- rough estimate,
- refined estimate,
- mistaken estimate,
- and corrigible estimate.

Truth is not abolished by this. It is approached through better and worse
estimation under repeated encounter.


## Estimates Can Be Shared

Because encodings need not be identical to be adequate, estimates can also be
shared without becoming identical.

This matters socially. Human loops live by exchanging estimates:

- a word,
- a gesture,
- a map,
- a warning,
- a theorem,
- a story,
- a sketch,
- a law,
- a work of art.

Each of these is a way of offering an organized estimate of what matters.

The receiving loop does not import it unchanged. It re-encodes it. But shared
life depends on the fact that loops can exchange estimates that are good enough
to orient one another toward the same world.

This is also why disagreement is not surprising. Shared recognition does not
require identical estimates. It requires estimates with enough overlap to keep
coordination, correction, and dialogue possible.


## Art and Science Are Refined Estimate Practices

This sharpens the earlier distinction from chapter 3.

Art and science are not opposites here. They are different practices for
producing, testing, and refining estimates.

Art refines estimates of salience, relation, mood, tone, tension, and lived
meaning. Science refines estimates of explicit relation, invariance, repeatable
distinction, and formal constraint.

Both are ways of returning to the same world with better organized recognition.

In that sense:

- art does not escape truth,
- science does not exhaust truth.

They are different estimate disciplines.


## Error Is a Bad Estimate, Not a Foreign Substance

Because knowing begins in estimate, error also has its proper place.

Error is not something alien invading an otherwise perfect recognizer. It is
what happens when an estimate:

- preserves too little,
- overextends too far,
- imports the wrong organization,
- or resists correction longer than it should.

So error is not outside truth-seeking. It is one of the ordinary risks of
finite recognition.

This is why correction matters so much. A good loop is not one that never
estimates wrongly. It is one that can revise its estimates under renewed
contact with what is.


## Selfhood Grows Through Better Estimation

This chapter also matters for the theory of self.

A self is not only a loop that recognizes. It is a loop that learns how much
weight to give its own estimates.

Too rigid, and it cannot be corrected.

Too unstable, and it cannot hold a world together long enough to act.

So maturation may be described, in part, as better estimation:

- better estimates of danger,
- better estimates of others,
- better estimates of consequence,
- better estimates of self,
- better estimates of which encodings deserve trust.

This is one reason recognition is evolutionary in a deep sense. The self
changes not only by receiving new patterns, but by learning how to estimate
them better.


## Estimate Does Not Relativize Truth

At this point, one mistake must be avoided.

To say that finite recognition begins in estimates is not to say that truth is
merely whatever any loop happens to estimate.

The world still resists. Patterns still return. Encodings still fail. Estimates
still collide with what is. Some estimates survive correction better than
others.

So estimate is not relativism.

It is the operational form taken by finite access to a world that exceeds any
single encounter and any single encoding.


## What This Chapter Commits To

This chapter commits only to the following:

- finite recognition begins in estimates rather than exhaustive capture;
- an estimate is a provisional organization sufficient for recognition and
  steering;
- some estimates are rough, others refined;
- estimates can be shared without becoming identical;
- art and science are different practices for refining estimates;
- error is a bad estimate, not a foreign substance;
- selfhood grows partly by learning how to estimate better.

That is enough for now.

The next step is close at hand. If loops live by estimate, then we must ask how
they correct one another, borrow one another's estimates, and sometimes achieve
shared recognition without collapsing into sameness.
